Levels of Analysis (Waltz): Distinguishing Systemic, Domestic, and Individual Drivers

1. Methodological Genesis & Core Logic

Levels of Analysis originated in Kenneth Waltz’s Man, the State, and War (1959), systematizing how International Relations scholars attribute causality to outcomes. Waltz argued that explanations fall into three analytically distinct categories—often conflated in practice.

  • Space Translation: Space policy outcomes emerge from interactions across all three levels: individual decisions by agency directors or CEOs (Musk’s Starship obsession), state-level institutional structures (China’s centralized space command vs. U.S. fragmented agencies), and systemic constraints (orbital mechanics, international treaty architecture). The challenge is diagnostic—when a space program fails or succeeds, which level deserves analytical primacy?
  • Epistemological Function: Produces causal disambiguation—clarifying whether an outcome stems from personality (replaceable), institutions (reformable), or system structure (immutable without multilateral action).
  • Core Logic: Analysts default to monocausal explanations (blaming individuals or “the system”). Waltz forces structured consideration of alternative hypotheses at each level, preventing premature closure on a single explanation.

2. Structural Components

The framework operates through three nested levels of causality, each with distinct ontological assumptions:

Level I: Individual (First Image)

  • Core Assumption: Human nature, psychology, and leadership choices drive outcomes
  • Space Manifestation:
    • Personality-Driven Programs: Musk’s Mars colonization fixation shapes SpaceX priorities; Bezos’s patient capital approach defines Blue Origin pace
    • Cognitive Biases: NASA Administrator’s risk aversion after Challenger/Columbia tragedies constrains human spaceflight decisions
    • Charismatic Authority: Wernher von Braun’s persuasive power secured Apollo funding; no equivalent figure exists today
  • Analytical Tools: Biography, psychological profiling, decision-making process tracing
  • Limitation: Over-personalization—attributing systemic outcomes to individual agency (the “Great Man” fallacy)

Level II: State/Domestic (Second Image)

  • Core Assumption: Internal characteristics of states (regime type, institutions, political culture) shape external behavior
  • Space Manifestation:
    • Regime Type: Democracies experience budget volatility (election cycles); autocracies sustain long-term investments (China’s 30-year lunar program)
    • Institutional Fragmentation: U.S. space governance divided across NASA, Space Force, NRO, FAA, NOAA—coordination failures vs. China’s unified command
    • Political Culture: Russian space program reflects Soviet-era prestige orientation (crewed missions prioritized over robotic efficiency)
    • Economic Structure: NewSpace boom in U.S. reflects venture capital ecosystem absent in European “Old Space” model
  • Analytical Tools: Comparative politics, bureaucratic politics models, organizational analysis
  • Limitation: Understates international constraints—even optimal domestic institutions cannot overcome systemic limits (e.g., U.S. cannot unilaterally create orbital debris removal regime)

Level III: System (Third Image)

  • Core Assumption: Structure of the international system (anarchy, polarity, distribution of capabilities) determines outcomes
  • Space Manifestation:
    • Anarchy: No supranational authority governs space; self-help dominates (nations develop independent launch to avoid dependencies)
    • Polarity: Cold War bipolarity (U.S.-USSR) drove competitive investment; post-Cold War unipolarity (U.S. dominance) reduced urgency; emerging multipolarity (U.S.-China-Russia-India) reignites competition
    • Capability Distribution: Offense-defense balance in space (ASATs cheaper than satellite defenses) incentivizes first-strike instability
    • Structural Constraints: Physics (delta-v budgets), geography (launch site latitudes), commons (finite GEO slots)
  • Analytical Tools: Structural realism, game theory, systems analysis
  • Limitation: Determinism—reduces agency to structural constraints (neglects how individual/domestic factors shape how states respond to system pressures)

Critical Insight: Levels are analytically distinct but empirically intertwined. A space outcome (e.g., Artemis delays) can have causes at all three levels: Trump’s shifting priorities (Individual), NASA’s risk-averse culture (State), and insufficient international cost-sharing (System). The error is privileging one level without testing alternatives.

3. Deployment Protocol

Pre-requisites:

  • Historical data on decision-making processes (meeting minutes, memoirs, budget documents)
  • Comparative cases (to distinguish idiosyncratic from systemic patterns)
  • Willingness to tolerate explanatory ambiguity (often no single level dominates)

Execution Sequence:

Phase I: Outcome Definition (Week 1)

  • Precisely specify the phenomenon requiring explanation:
    • Example: “Why did China accelerate lunar program development 2015-2025?”
  • Avoid vague formulations (“Why is space more competitive?")—levels of analysis require specific dependent variables

Phase II: Level I Hypothesis Generation (Week 2)

  • Identify key decision-makers and their preferences/constraints:
    • Example: Xi Jinping’s emphasis on “space dream” as component of national rejuvenation (personal ideology)
    • Data Required: Speeches, policy directives, resource allocation patterns under Xi vs. predecessors
  • Hypothesis Test: Would outcome differ under alternative leadership? (If no, Level I has weak explanatory power)

Phase III: Level II Hypothesis Generation (Week 3-4)

  • Map domestic institutional landscape:
    • Example: China’s 2015 military reforms centralized space assets under Strategic Support Force—removing interservice rivalry
    • Compare: U.S. created Space Force 2019 but Army/Navy retain satellite control—fragmentation persists
  • Hypothesis Test: Do states with similar domestic structures exhibit similar space behaviors? (If yes, Level II gains support)

Phase IV: Level III Hypothesis Generation (Week 5-6)

  • Assess systemic pressures:
    • Example: U.S. withdrawal from ISS cooperation (post-2024 planned exit) created systemic opening for China to become alternative hub
    • Structural Logic: Bipolar competition (U.S.-China) incentivizes both to secure prestige projects (Apollo-Sputnik analog)
  • Hypothesis Test: Do states across different regime types respond similarly to systemic pressures? (If yes, Level III dominates)

Phase V: Explanatory Weighting (Week 7)

  • Rank levels by explanatory contribution (not exclusive causality):
    • Example Weighting:
      • Level I (Xi’s ideology): 20%
      • Level II (institutional reform): 40%
      • Level III (U.S.-China competition): 40%
  • Critical Decision: Can one level be eliminated without undermining the explanation? If yes, it’s secondary.

Phase VI: Counterfactual Testing (Week 8)

  • For top-ranked level, develop counterfactuals:
    • If Level II dominant: “Would China’s lunar acceleration occur if Xi led but institutional reforms hadn’t happened?” (Test: Yes = Level I dominates; No = Level II validated)
    • If Level III dominant: “Would acceleration occur under different Chinese leader but same U.S. competition?” (Test: Yes = Level III dominates; No = Level I/II matter)

Output Format: Multi-Level Causal Analysis with:

  • Hypothesis inventory (all three levels)
  • Evidence mapping (which data supports which hypothesis)
  • Explanatory weighting (percentage contribution per level)
  • Policy implications (which level is actionable for your organization?)

4. Failure Modes & Constraints

Cognitive Biases:

  • Level I Overattribution: Media narratives personalize outcomes (“Musk’s genius” or “NASA administrator’s incompetence”)—neglects structural constraints
  • Level III Determinism: Structural realists dismiss agency (“System structure compels all states to behave identically”)—neglects domestic variation
  • False Precision: Assigning percentage weights (30% Level I, 70% Level III) implies quantifiable causality where none exists

Invalidation Conditions:

  • Unique Events: Levels of Analysis requires comparative cases; singular events (first Moon landing) lack comparison set
  • Rapid Change: Framework assumes relatively stable levels; revolutionary disruption (SpaceX reusability breakthrough) may originate at one level but cascade across all
  • Non-State Actors: Waltz designed framework for state behavior; commercial space (SpaceX, Blue Origin) doesn’t fit neatly into state/system dichotomy

Misuse Pattern: Using levels of analysis to avoid making causal claims (“It’s complicated, all three levels matter”). Framework should clarify causality, not obscure it. Force rank-ordering even if uncomfortable.

5. Integration Points

Upstream Feeder:

  • PESTLE Analysis (1.1): PESTLE factors map onto Waltz levels (Political/Economic/Legal = Level II domestic; Technological = Level I/II; Environmental can be Level III systemic)
  • Causal Layered Analysis (1.3): CLA’s Worldview/Myth layers often correspond to Level II (national political culture)

Downstream Amplifier:

  • Scenario Planning (5.1): Different scenarios can test which level dominates (Scenario A: charismatic leader emerges [Level I]; Scenario B: institutional reform [Level II]; Scenario C: systemic polarity shift [Level III])
  • War-gaming (4.3): Game outcomes reveal whether individual decisions, domestic constraints, or systemic structure drive results

Synergistic Pairing:

  • Alliance Relationship Analysis (3.1): Alliance cohesion can be explained at all three levels (individual leader relationships, domestic institutional compatibility, systemic threat perception)

Sequential Logic: Levels of Analysis (causal diagnosis) → SWOT (assess which levels are actionable) → Stakeholder Mapping (identify actors at each level)

6. Exemplar Case

Context: Explaining U.S. Space Force creation (2019)—why did the U.S. establish first new military service in 72 years?

Multi-Level Analysis:

Level I Hypothesis: Trump’s Personal Priority

  • Evidence:
    • Trump repeatedly advocated for Space Force at rallies (personal branding)
    • Previous administrations (Obama, Bush) rejected similar proposals
    • Trump’s transactional worldview (“We need to dominate space like we dominate Earth”)
  • Test: Would Space Force exist without Trump?
    • Counterfactual: Hillary Clinton presidency (2017-2021)—Clinton’s 2016 platform mentioned space but not military reorganization
    • Conclusion: Trump was necessary but perhaps not sufficient (other factors mattered)

Level II Hypothesis: Institutional Momentum

  • Evidence:
    • Air Force Space Command advocated for independence since 1990s (bureaucratic entrepreneurship)
    • 2001 Rumsfeld Commission recommended protecting space assets (pre-Trump)
    • Bipartisan congressional support (proposal passed with Democratic votes)
    • Analogy: Air Force separated from Army in 1947 not due to Truman’s personality, but technological evolution (airpower maturity)
  • Test: Do other nations exhibit similar institutional trajectories?
    • Comparative: France created Space Command (2019), Germany (2021), UK (2021)—all without Trump-equivalent leaders
    • Conclusion: Level II provides cross-national explanatory power (Trump-centric explanation fails for France/Germany/UK)

Level III Hypothesis: Systemic Competition

  • Evidence:
    • China’s 2015 military reorganization created Strategic Support Force (space/cyber/electronic warfare)
    • Russia demonstrated ASAT capabilities (2020 test)
    • Growing space debris threat (Kessler Syndrome risk)
    • Security dilemma: As adversaries develop counter-space, U.S. must organize to deter/respond
  • Test: Do adversaries perceive U.S. Space Force as response to their actions?
    • Reactions: Russia condemned Space Force as “militarization” (implicitly acknowledging it as response to Russian capabilities)
    • China’s 2019 defense white paper cited U.S. space developments as threat
    • Conclusion: Systemic action-reaction dynamic evident

Explanatory Weighting (Analyst Assessment):

  • Level I (Trump): 30%—accelerated timeline, shaped branding, but underlying momentum existed
  • Level II (Institutions): 40%—Air Force Space Command’s decades-long advocacy, bipartisan support
  • Level III (System): 30%—China/Russia developments created threat perception justifying reorganization

Critical Insight: Initial media coverage attributed Space Force 100% to Trump (Level I bias). Deeper analysis reveals Trump was catalyst for an institutional trajectory (Level II) responding to systemic pressures (Level III) that predated and will outlast his presidency.

Policy Implication by Level:

  • If Level I dominant: Space Force vulnerable to reversal under future presidents → institutionalize rapidly
  • If Level II dominant: Focus on internal reorganization, reduce interservice rivalry → bureaucratic reform
  • If Level III dominant: Space Force is inevitable response to adversary actions → prepare for escalatory dynamics

Red Team Critique: Analysis may underweight Level III. If space competition intensifies (hypothetical Chinese lunar base with military-dual use), Space Force reorganization will appear obviously necessary in retrospect—suggesting systemic pressures were more determinative than initially assessed. Beware hindsight bias in level weighting.

Practitioner Warning: Levels of Analysis is a diagnostic tool, not a predictive model. It clarifies what happened and why but cannot reliably forecast what will happen. For prediction, combine with Scenario Planning (testing how different level dominance produces alternative futures). Additionally, recognize that Waltz’s framework privileges state actors—commercial space (SpaceX, Blue Origin) requires conceptual extension beyond classical IR theory.
